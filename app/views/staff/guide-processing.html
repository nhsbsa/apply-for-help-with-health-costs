Introduction

Help with fees makes it possible for people who aren't able to pay their court or tribunal fee to get financial help. Applicants are assessed on the basis of their financial situation.

As you process an application for help with fees, you'll see prompts and explanations on the right hand side of the screen. You can also return to this page if you need more help.

The service has a link to the Department for Work and Pensions and can check whether applicants are receiving benefits.

When you’ve entered all the information from the form you’ll get a result about whether the applicant is eligible for help with fees.

You’ll also be prompted to send a letter requesting further information or part-payment if the applicant hasn’t fully completed the form, or has to pay part of the fee.


Who can apply for help with fees?

Individuals can apply for help with fees if they:
  • only have a small amount of savings and investments
  • and receive certain benefits
  • or are on a low income

Before you start

Check the statement of truth has been signed

Unsigned forms must be returned to the applicant.

Check the applicant is not a company, charity or not for profit organisation

Help with fees is generally only available to individuals; the scheme does not apply to other types of business, companies or charities. There are some exceptions:

  • sole traders; this is someone trading as an individual
  • companies applying to the Gambling Appeals Tribunal; companies applying to this tribunal should use separate forms and guidance for help with fees
  • charities and not for profit organisations making a case to the UK Supreme Court

Check the applicant is not one of the following:
  • on the list of vexatious litigants banned from starting court proceedings without permission
  • bound by a general civil restraint order (CRO) to stop them from bringing certain cases to court
  • bound by an extended civil restraint order (CRO) to stop them making applications in certain courts or make further court claims
  • bound by a restrictions of proceedings order (RPO)
    • that limits the cases that can be brought by the applicant
  • someone other than the person who must pay the fee; third parties may not apply for help with fees unless they're acting as a litigation friend, parent or guardian

Check the fee:
  • was not processed through the money claim online (MCOL) or possession claim online (PCOL) services
  • is not for a search or request for duplicate documents (unless the applicant did not receive the originals or had no fixed address when an order was made)

Online applications

We’re trialling a new service for the public to apply for help with court fees online; soon it will be available to everyone in the UK.

The new online service asks the same questions as the existing ‘apply for help with fees’ form (EX160) and you’ll be able to process it in the same way that you’ve been processing paper applications for help with fees.

  1. everyone who applies for help with fees online will get a reference number, like this: HWF-1612-1345
  2. you’ll find the reference number on the court or tribunal form in the HWF reference number field, or in a letter if the applicant is applying for a refund
  3. enter this reference number in the ‘Process an online application field’. It's important to enter the dashes in the reference number exactly as they appear in the example above
  4. check the name of the applicant is correct and enter the following details: fee; jurisdiction; and form name or number. You can also select whether it’s an emergency case
  5. check all the details you’ve entered are correct on the ‘Check details page’ and click ‘Complete processing’.
  6. you’ll see the outcome of the application together with the reference number on the final page. Enter the application reference number into the case management system as usual

Not all offices are receiving online applications yet. We'll let you know when the online service is available in your area.

People applying from outside the UK

You can accept applications from people living outside the UK if their case is being dealt with by a court or tribunal in the UK.

You can still accept an application from someone who has ‘no recourse to public funds’ specified on their visa. They will be assessed in the same way as any other foreign national or non-resident UK citizen.

Make sure the application uses UK pounds sterling for details of income.


National Insurance number

If NI number isn't provided:
  • Check answer to question 9
  • If 'No', continue to process without NI number
  • If 'Yes', don't process and contact applicant by phone to ask for their NI number

If you're unable to contact the applicant by phone, you should return the application and ask them to provide their National Insurance number or a letter confirming that they're receiving benefits.



Status

There are some exceptions to the standard definition of 'single' for someone without a partner who relies on their own income, for example when the case involves:

  • divorce, dissolution or annulment*
  • gender recognition applications
  • domestic violence
  • forced marriage
  • where a couple are both part of a 'multiple fee' group

* If an applicant is involved in a divorce, dissolution or annulment case they should be considered to be part of a couple if they've formed a new relationship where they're living together and sharing a joint income.

Married or living with someone and sharing an income

Applicants should still be considered to be married or living with someone and sharing an income even if they're not currently living at the same address. For example if one or both are:

  • serving in the Armed forces
  • are in prison
  • living in residential care


Refunds

Check the refund:

  • is for a fee paid less than 3 months ago
  • hasn't already been refunded

If the fee was paid more than 3 months ago, your delivery manager may be able to extend the period of 3 months if they think there's good reason to do so.


Emergency cases

An emergency case is one where delay may harm the applicant or the applicant’s case.

Example of emergency cases:
  • suspending an eviction
  • debtor insolvency petition
  • children or vulnerable adults
  • domestic violence
  • injunctions
  • ‘out of hours’ provisions at the Royal Courts of Justice

In some situations there's no time for the applicant to complete a help with fees form before their emergency case is heard.

The delivery manager can ask the applicant (or their legal representative) for an 'undertaking' to apply for help with fees or pay the fee within 7 days.

Applicants providing an undertaking must complete form EX160B

Benefits

If an applicant is applying for help with fees based on receiving benefits, you’ll receive confirmation from the Department for Work and Pensions that they’re receiving one of these 6 benefits:

  • Income-based Jobseeker's Allowance (JSA)
  • Income-related Employment and Support Allowance (ESA)
  • Income Support
  • Universal Credit (and you’re earning less than £6,000)
  • State Pension (Guarantee Credit)
  • Scottish Civil Legal Aid (not Advice and Assistance or Advice by Way of Representation)


Total monthly income

The service will calculate whether the applicant is eligible for help based on how much they earn, whether there are any dependent children and if they’re part of a couple.

If the applicant is part of a couple:
  • they must have provided details of their partner’s income
  • add the 2 monthly income amounts and enter the total
  • when you’ve entered the total monthly income you’ll see whether the applicant is eligible for help

What shouldn't be included as income

If the applicant includes any of these benefits as part of their income, then you will need to subtract the amount received from their monthly total.

  • Armed Forces Independence Payment (AFIP)
  • Attendance Allowance
  • Back to Work Bonus
  • Bereavement Allowance
  • Budgeting Advances paid under Universal Credit
  • Budgeting Loan
  • Carer’s Allowance
  • Carer Element of Universal Credit
  • Childcare Element of Working Tax Credit
  • Childcare Element of Universal Credit
  • Cold Weather Payment
  • Constant Attendance Allowance
  • Direct payments made under Community Care, Services for Carer and Children’s Services
  • Disability Living Allowance (DLA)
  • Disabled and Severely Disabled elements of Child Tax Credit
  • Disabled and Severely Disabled Child elements of Working Tax Credit
  • Disabled and Severely Disabled Child elements of Universal Credit
  • Exceptionally Severe Disablement Allowance
  • Financial support under an agreement for the foster care of a child
  • Funeral Payment
  • Housing Benefit
  • Housing Credit Element of Pension Credit
  • Housing Element of Universal Credit
  • Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit
  • Independent Living Fund payments
  • Limited Capability for Work Element of Universal Credit
  • Personal Independence Payment (PIP)
  • Any pension paid under the Naval, Military and Air forces etc (Disablement and Death) service Pension Order 2006
  • Severe Disablement Allowance
  • Short Term Benefit Advances (STBAs )
  • Universal Credit Advances
  • Widowed Parent’s Allowance
